Case Study: The Anatomy of a Ransomware Attack
Definition:
Ransomware is a type of malicious software (malware) designed to block access to a computer system or encrypt its data until a sum of money (the "ransom") is paid. Modern variants often include "double extortion," where hackers also threaten to leak sensitive data publicly if the payment isn't met.

How it Happens (Step-by-Step):

Infiltration: The attack begins when a user clicks a malicious link in a phishing email or downloads an infected attachment. It can also occur through unsecured Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP) ports.

Deployment: Once inside the network, the malware "calls home" to a Command-and-Control (C2) server to receive instructions and encryption keys.

Lateral Movement: The virus silently spreads through the local network, seeking out high-value targets like database servers and backup drives.

Encryption: The software executes a complex cryptographic algorithm, locking all files. Users find their documents replaced by unreadable formats (e.g., .locked or .encrypted).

The Demand: A "Ransom Note" appears on the screen, providing instructions on how to pay in cryptocurrency (like Bitcoin) to receive the decryption key.

Target Audience:
While individuals are targeted, hackers primarily focus on "Big Game" targets: healthcare providers, educational institutions (like universities), and government agencies. These organizations are targeted because they rely on 24/7 data availability and are more likely to pay to avoid operational downtime.

Consequences:
The impact is often devastating. Beyond the financial loss of the ransom (which can range from thousands to millions of dollars), organizations face massive data recovery costs, legal penalties for data breaches, and long-term reputational damage. For critical sectors like hospitals, the consequence can even be the loss of human life due to delayed treatments.
